What does fabric count mean?

The "count" is how many stitches fit in one inch of fabric. A 100-stitch-wide design on 14-count fabric comes out about 7.1" wide; the same design on 18-count shrinks to about 5.6" — smaller stitches, same chart. Higher counts give finer detail but demand more precision and better lighting.

FAQ

How much extra fabric do I need for framing?

At least 3 inches per side — 6 inches added to each dimension in total. Go to 4 inches per side if you plan a scroll frame or a wide mat.

Can I use this for evenweave or linen?

Yes. Evenweave and linen are usually stitched "over two" threads, so divide the fabric count by two: 28-count over two behaves like 14-count Aida. Our dropdown handles this automatically.

Do fractional stitches change the size?

No. Quarter, half, and three-quarter stitches add detail inside the grid — a 100×100 pattern stays 100×100 cells regardless.

What is the difference between Aida and evenweave?

Aida has a visible grid of holes that makes counting easy. Evenweave is a finer, more uniform weave that looks like regular fabric — often chosen when unstitched fabric will show in the finished piece.

How speed changes your timeline

Ranges account for ±25% speed uncertainty. Confetti-heavy patterns run at the high end — check your FLOW Score in the editor.

What is a realistic stitching speed?

Most stitchers settle between 100 and 160 stitches per hour at a comfortable pace. Beginners typically start around 60–80 while building muscle memory for counting and tension, and experienced stitchers using a stand and railroading techniques can sustain 250+. Burst speeds of 350–400 happen on big single-colour blocks, but they never survive contact with a real chart full of colour changes.

Want your personal number? Time one focused 30-minute session, count the completed stitches, and double it.

Why do projects always take longer than estimated?

Naive estimates divide stitch count by speed and stop there — that only measures needle-in-fabric time. Real projects include thread changes (30–60 seconds each, hundreds of times), chart-checking, breaks, and the occasional frogging session. That overhead typically adds 30–50% to a project, which is why this calculator applies a multiplier (×1.4 by default) instead of pretending it away.

Scattered single stitches — confetti — are the biggest time sink of all: each one costs a full thread change for one or two crosses. Two patterns with identical stitch counts can differ by 40+ hours purely on confetti. Patternly's Check tab measures exactly this with the FLOW Score and ConfettiScope, and the Confetti Cleanup tool fixes it before you commit.

How to stitch faster

Use a stand. Freeing both hands is the single biggest speed upgrade most stitchers ever make. Fix your lighting — a daylight LED lamp makes counting faster and colours easier to tell apart. Work cross-country through colour blocks (finish one colour in an area before switching), and reduce confetti before you start: cutting a photo conversion from 40 colours to 20–25 often removes most scattered singles while keeping the picture readable.

FAQ

What overhead multiplier should I pick?

×1.4 is a sensible default for a normal pattern. Use ×1.2 for designs with large single-colour blocks and few colours; ×1.5 or more for confetti-heavy conversions with 30+ colours.

Does fabric count change how long it takes?

The stitch count is the same, so the theoretical time is too — but most stitchers report being 20–30% slower on 18-count than 14-count because the smaller holes demand more precision.

Is consistency or speed more important?

Consistency, by far. Thirty minutes every day finishes more projects than an eight-hour binge once a month — and your tension stays more even, too.

Does backstitch count toward the estimate?

No — backstitch, French knots, and specialty stitches aren't in the cross-stitch count, so a pattern with heavy outlining will run over the estimate. Budget extra time for it.

How much floss is in a skein?

A standard Luca-S, DMC, or Anchor skein holds 8 metres of 6-strand floss; Madeira skeins hold 10 metres. Because you separate the strands before stitching, the usable length depends on how many strands you pull:

Strands usedBundles per skeinUsable length (8 m skein)
1 strand648 m
2 strands324 m
3 strands216 m
6 strands18 m

Why the 20% safety margin?

Real thread goes places calculators ignore: 2–3 cm of every cut length disappears into anchoring at the start and end, frogged thread is rarely reusable, and confetti stitches burn short runs with constant start/stop cycles. Most importantly, dye lots vary — a replacement skein bought months later can be a subtly different shade that shows in the finished piece. Buying all skeins of a colour at once, with margin, is the safe play.

How long should I cut my thread?

The classic answer is 18 inches (45 cm) — fingertips to elbow. Shorter lengths tangle less and stay fresher; longer lengths thin and fuzz as they pass through the fabric dozens of times. Go shorter for metallics and dark colours on light fabric. If you use the loop-start method, cut double (36") and fold — it only works with even strand counts.

FAQ

How many stitches does one skein cover?

On 14-count with 2 strands, roughly 960 full crosses per 8 m skein. On 18-count that rises to about 1,240 because each stitch is smaller. The table above adjusts for your exact settings.

Do I calculate backstitch separately?

Yes — backstitch usually uses 1 strand and consumes about 2 cm of thread per 1 cm of drawn line. Heavy outlining can add 10–20% to a colour's total.

Should I buy extra of dominant colours?

Yes. Backgrounds, skies, and skin tones are where a mid-project dye-lot switch is most visible. One extra skein of each dominant colour is cheap insurance.
